Faster than the Wind - an Animated Explainer

by William Wu · 5 minute read

One day, a close friend of mine sent me this video by Veritasium:

In this video, Derek (the channel's protagonist) tries out an experimental propeller craft that claims to travel faster than the wind that pushes it. How is this possible?

Let's make sure we fully understand what's going on. Consider this propellor car next to a sail car in a race. The sail car travels at the wind's speed, which should allow us to compare them if we start them at the same spot.

Mouseover the diagram to play the animation. Your mouse is the wind!

The propeller car won the race! Whereas the sail only allows the sail car to travel at the speed of the wind and no faster, the propeller car gains some kind of advantage from its propeller, allowing it to travel faster than the wind pushing it.

Energy to push the car doesn't just come out of thin air (haha, get it?). A propeller by itself would just spin freely in the wind, so the magic must be in how it's somehow connected to the wheels and thus the ground.

Now let's pause for a moment. Instead of a propellor rotating towards and away from us in “fake 3D”, let's just replace it with a purely 2D version that rotates in a way that we can see more clearly.

This new style of propeller works like a watermill, but with half of it covered to keep the wind spinning it the right way. Even though it looks different, it's function is the same: to convert wind energy into rotation and vice versa.

Now let's try coupling this new windmill to the wheels. Instead of using a belt like the propeller craft, let's just enlarge the wheel and just connect it directly. Voila!

Uh oh. Looks like this car is travelling at only half the speed of the wind and no faster. Why is that? Let's take a closer look at the forces involved.

Together, the windmill and wheel acts like a second-class lever! This lever lets the wind push the car with twice the force, as indicated by the arrows. This 2x factor in force is also known as our Mechanical Advantage. However, the force is applied over half the distance, which ultimately causes our car to travel more slowly.

Let's simply this to an equivalent pulley system.

Intuitively, energy is conserved. We're just trading less distance for more force. However, if we want our car to go faster than the wind, we want the opposite tradeoff: we want more distance and less force (as long as we can still overcome wind resistance).

The easiest way to achieve this is to run the pulley in reverse. Input becomes output, turning our Mechanical Advantage from 2 to 1/2.

Intuitively, if you were pulling this blue string, it would feel harder than just pulling the black string directly. But you're rewarded by this difficulty as the black string moves towards you more quickly.

But what does this look like in our wind car? With a belt attached to the propellor, you could just attach a belt the other way around, but with a windmill you have to get creative. And that means shrinking the windmill and mounting it upside down.

It works, but why? Let's take a closer look.

Mounting the windmill upside down puts the force of the wind between the car and the ground, effectively creating a third class lever with a mechanical advantage less than 1. When the wind pushes on this lever, the car responds with less force, but over a greater distance – going further than the wind itself.

If you want a real, tangible, hands on example, you could try to set up a similar system at home. Grab a spool of thread and hot glue two CD's to the ends. You could use toilet paper, LEGOs, whatever you have at hand. What matters is that the spool is wound underhand and has larger diameter wheels attached.

When you pull on the thread, your hand is acting like the wind. The thread should grow shorter and shorter, meaning that the contraption is rolling towards you faster than you can pull! In other words, if the contraption were the propeller car, it would be moving faster than the wind that's pushing it.

In summary, it's totally possible for a car to travel faster than the wind without breaking the laws of physics. Adding a propeller doesn't magically generate energy. Rather, by attaching propeller to the wheels in a specific way, we change how the energy between wind and ground is applied to the car - using mechanical advantage to trade less force for more distance.

Some problems are easier solved when they are visualized. I love using analogies that unlock intuition that we might not realize we have like windmills, levers, and pulleys. Hopefully you're able to walk away from this article feeling like you've learned something.

Extra notes for the physics hungry

A disclaimer for those out there who study physics: I'm not a physics major nor did I study aerodynamics, so please, if you spot any errors or corrections please contribute to my learning and let me know. With that said, I do have a few things to (slightly) formalize and expand on.

In an mathematically ideal case, mechanical advantage affects two things. Firstly, mechanical advantage affect's a car's theoretical max speed the lower the MA, the faster the max speed. Secondly, mechanical advantage affects the forces applied to a car. From a standstill, a lower MA results in less force and thus less acceleration and longer time needed to get up to speed. Now assuming a finite wind speed and a finite mass car, the car can travel infinitely fast as the mechanical advantage approaches zero. At that point, the infinitesimally small force would require waiting infinitely long for the car to get up to such infinite speeds (but it will!).

In the practical case, lowering mechanical advantage does help us go faster... up to a point. Even from a standstill, there will be static/kinetic friction caused by the wheels, belt drive, and bearings. As we speed up, we encounter aerodynamic drag from the propeller and from the car body itself. In order to keep moving, the output force after mechanical advantage must outweigh friction and drag, putting an limit on how low our mechanical advantage can go.

This gives us a kind of curve to try to hit. As we lower our MA starting from 1, our maximum top speed increases, but our maximum force decreases until we hit our drag+friction enforced lower limit at which point we stop accelerating. Somewhere between 1 and the lower limit is the optimal MA to get the best of both worlds – a reasonable top speed with enough force to get there in reasonable time.

This optimal MA point isn't just a single point; it's a moving target that changes as we speed up due to the effect of drag on our lower limit. This makes it important to be able to change MA on the fly. There are many factors that affect our MA including the wheel diameter and belt ratio, but not all of these are easy to adjust in real time. The propeller car in Veritasium's video features a controllable pitch angle for the propellor, which is used to tune the MA to get it in the right spot.

However, I noticed there's a point in the video around the 4:07 mark where Derek asks how to slow down the wind car, and the answer is vague. One person claims to increase the pitch, another claims to decrease the pitch, until someone says “feel which is slowing you down and do that”. In the context of the ideal MA, this makes a lot of sense, as depending on where you are on the MA curve, slowing down could mean different things, but pushing to either extreme will definitely slow you down.

I wish I had the expertise to plot out this graph, but I would imagine it looks similar to torque charts for gearboxes. If you speed up while biking, it's important to change gears so that you can continue to pedal comfortably by changing the gearing, you're changing the mechanical advantage to maintain a sweet spot between force, top speed, and drag. Most of this we do intuitively, until it feels right.
